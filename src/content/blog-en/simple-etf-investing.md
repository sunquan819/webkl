Most people think investing is complicated. You need to analyze stocks, time the market, pick winners.

That's wrong.

Regular people can invest simply and effectively. ETFs make it easy.

## My Simple Approach

Here's exactly what I do. Nothing fancy.

**Step 1: Pick One or Two ETFs**

Don't overthink this. One broad ETF is enough.

I use VOO. It tracks the S&P 500 - 500 largest US companies. One fund, instant diversification.

Alternatively, VTI for total US market. Or a target-date fund if you want even simpler.

That's it. One fund. Done.

**Step 2: Invest Every Month**

Every month, same day, same amount.

$500, $1000, whatever you can afford. Set up automatic transfer, forget about it.

This is dollar-cost averaging. You buy more shares when prices are low, fewer when high. Over time, you get average prices.

No timing the market. No stress about when to buy.

**Step 3: Ignore the News**

Market crashed? Keep buying.

Market at all-time high? Keep buying.

Economy in recession? Keep buying.

Headlines will always be scary. Economists will always predict doom. Ignore all of it.

Your job: invest consistently. The market's job: go up over time.

Historically, the S&P 500 returns about 10% annually. Not every year, but over decades.

## Why This Works

Let me explain the logic.

**Diversification:**

VOO holds 500 companies. If one goes bankrupt, you have 499 others.

You're not betting on any single company. You're betting on the US economy long-term.

**Low Fees:**

VOO charges 0.03% annually. That's $3 per $10,000 invested.

Many mutual funds charge 1% or more. That's $100 per $10,000.

Over 30 years, that difference is massive. Fees compound against you.

**Simplicity:**

No research needed. No analyzing financial statements. No watching CNBC.

Buy the ETF, hold forever. Check your balance once a year.

Time saved: hours per week. Stress saved: immeasurable.

**Historical Performance:**

S&P 500 has never had a negative 20-year period. Ever.

In the short-term, markets are volatile. In the long-term, they rise.

Your timeframe: decades. Who cares about short-term swings?

## What About Bonds?

Younger people can be 100% stocks.

As you get older, add bonds for stability.

Simple rule: your age in bonds.

30 years old? 30% bonds, 70% stocks.
50 years old? 50% bonds, 50% stocks.

For bonds, use BND or similar total bond market ETF.

## Common Questions

**When should I sell?**

Ideally, never. Hold until retirement.

If you need money for emergencies, that's what your emergency fund is for.

If you need money for a house, that should be in savings, not stocks.

Invest only money you won't need for 10+ years.

**What if the market crashes?**

Great! You get to buy more shares at lower prices.

Market crashes are opportunities for long-term investors.

Think about it: you're buying every month. Lower prices mean more shares. When prices recover, you have more shares worth more money.

Crashes only hurt if you sell. Don't sell.

**What about international stocks?**

Good question. US stocks have outperformed recently, but that might not continue.

Add VXUS for international exposure if you want diversification.

My allocation: 80% US, 20% international. But 100% US is fine too.

**How much should I invest?**

As much as you can afford.

Start with 10% of income if possible. Increase as income grows.

The earlier you start, the more time compound interest works for you.

$500/month at 8% return becomes $700,000 in 30 years. Starting early matters more than starting big.

## What I Don't Do

**Individual Stock Picking:**

I don't pick individual stocks. Too much work, too much risk.

Some people enjoy it. I don't. ETFs are easier.

**Market Timing:**

I don't try to buy low and sell high. Studies show most investors fail at timing.

My approach: buy consistently, hold forever.

**Complex Strategies:**

No options trading for long-term investments. No leverage. No day trading.

Simple strategies work. Complex strategies add risk.

**Sector Bets:**

I don't bet on specific sectors. Tech, energy, healthcare - I own them all through VOO.

Some people make sector bets. I don't have the skill or interest.

## Getting Started

Open a brokerage account. Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab - all fine.

Set up automatic monthly investments. Even $100 works.

Pick VOO or VTI. Start investing.

Increase contributions as income grows.

Check your balance once per year. Otherwise, ignore.

That's it. That's the whole method.

## The Boring Truth

Successful investing is boring.

No excitement. No drama. No brilliant predictions.

Just consistent contributions to boring index funds, year after year.

Boring makes money. Excitement loses money.

If your investing feels exciting, you're probably doing it wrong.
