People think travel is expensive. It doesn't have to be.

Last year I traveled to 6 countries, spent less than some people spend on a single resort vacation.

Here's how.

## The Biggest Expense: Flights

Flights can be your biggest cost or your smallest, depending on flexibility.

**Be Flexible on Dates:**

Flying on Tuesday instead of Friday can save 30-50%. Use Google Flights to see price calendars.

Red-eye flights are cheaper. Early morning flights are cheaper. Saturday flights are often cheaper.

If you can shift dates by a few days, check prices. You might save hundreds.

**Be Flexible on Destinations:**

Instead of "I want to go to Paris," think "I want to go to Europe."

Search for flights to anywhere in a region. Skyscanner has an "Everywhere" search.

Often found amazing deals to places I hadn't considered. Buda instead of Paris. Bangkok instead of Tokyo.

**Use Points and Miles:**

Credit card sign-up bonuses can fund flights. A good bonus is 50,000+ miles - enough for a round-trip.

I use travel rewards cards for daily spending. Points accumulate. Free flights result.

Warning: only do this if you pay your balance in full. Interest negates any benefits.

**Book in Advance, But Not Too Far:**

Domestic flights: book 1-3 months ahead.
International flights: book 2-6 months ahead.

Too early and prices are high. Too late and prices surge.

## Accommodation Hacks

Hotels are expensive. Alternatives exist.

**Hostels:**

Not just for young backpackers. Many hostels have private rooms.

I've stayed in hostels with private rooms for $30/night that would cost $100+ at hotels.

Clean, safe, social. Check reviews carefully.

**Airbnb:**

Good for longer stays or groups. Often cheaper than hotels.

But check all fees. Cleaning fees can make "cheap" listings expensive.

I use Airbnb for stays over 5 nights. Hotels for short stays.

**Location Matters:**

Stay outside city centers. Hotels in city center cost double or triple.

Stay 15-20 minutes from center by public transport. Save 50% on accommodation.

I stay in residential neighborhoods. More authentic, less touristy, cheaper food.

**Travel Off-Season:**

Peak season: everything costs double. Crowds everywhere.

Shoulder season: prices drop 30-50%. Weather still good. Fewer crowds.

For Europe: May and September instead of July and August.
For Southeast Asia: November and March instead of December and January.

## Eating Well for Less

Food is part of travel. You can eat well without spending a fortune.

**Eat Where Locals Eat:**

Tourist restaurants charge tourist prices. Walk 5 blocks away from attractions, find cheaper, better food.

In Thailand, I paid $2 for street food that locals ate. Tourist restaurants: $10 for worse food.

**Cook Sometimes:**

If staying in Airbnb or hostel with kitchen, cook some meals.

Breakfast especially. I buy groceries for breakfast, eat out for lunch and dinner.

Saves money, saves time, sometimes healthier.

**Lunch Over Dinner:**

Many restaurants have lunch specials. Same food, lower price.

I eat big lunches out, light dinners. Or buy dinner from grocery stores.

**Avoid Hotel Breakfasts:**

Hotel breakfasts often cost $20-30. Not worth it.

Find a local bakery or cafe. Pay $5. Often better anyway.

## Transportation Tips

**Public Transport:**

Taxis and ride-shares add up. Learn public transport.

Most cities have transit passes. Weekly passes often cheaper than individual tickets.

In Europe, trains connect cities affordably. In Asia, buses are ultra-cheap.

**Walk:**

Walking is free. And you see more.

I walk everywhere within 2-3 kilometers. Good exercise, better experience.

**Rent Bikes:**

Many cities have bike-sharing. Faster than walking, cheaper than taxis.

Amsterdam, Copenhagen, Paris - biking is the best way to see these cities.

## Best Budget Destinations

Some places give you more for your money.

**Southeast Asia:**

Thailand, Vietnam, Indonesia. $30-50/day covers nice accommodation, good food, activities.

Flights might be expensive depending where you live. But once there, costs are low.

**Eastern Europe:**

Poland, Hungary, Czech Republic. Beautiful cities, great food, fraction of Western Europe prices.

Prague, Budapest, Krakow - stunning architecture, vibrant culture, $60-80/day.

**Portugal:**

Western Europe but cheaper. Lisbon, Porto - great food, nice weather, reasonable prices.

**Mexico:**

For North Americans, Mexico is close and cheap. Beaches, cities, food - all affordable.

$50-70/day goes far.

**Turkey:**

Incredible history, amazing food, very affordable. Istanbul is one of my favorite cities.

The lira has weakened, making it very cheap for visitors.

## What I Don't Pay For

**Tourist Traps:**

Overpriced, underwhelming attractions. Skip them.

Do I need to see every museum? No. I pick one or two interesting ones, skip the rest.

**Guided Tours:**

Sometimes worth it. Often not.

I research online, plan my own tours. Free walking tours exist in most cities. They work on tips.

**Travel Insurance That Doesn't Cover Anything:**

I buy travel insurance. But I read the fine print.

Cheapest isn't always best. Make sure it covers what you need - medical, cancellations, lost luggage.

**Currency Exchange at Airports:**

Worst rates anywhere.

Use ATMs. Or exchange before traveling. Airport rates are a ripoff.

**Souvenirs:**

I buy almost no souvenirs. They're overpriced and collect dust.

My souvenirs are photos and memories. Free and don't clutter my home.

## Daily Budget Examples

**Budget Travel:**

$30-50/day in Southeast Asia or Eastern Europe. Hostels, street food, public transport.

**Mid-Range:**

$70-100/day in Western Europe. Airbnb or budget hotels, mix of restaurants and self-catering.

**My Typical Trip:**

2-week trip to Portugal:
Flights: $600 (booked early)
Accommodation: $500 (Airbnb, 14 nights)
Food: $350 (mix of restaurants and self-catering)
Transport: $100 (trains and buses)
Activities: $150 (museums, day trips)
Total: $1700 for 2 weeks, $85/day

Not luxury, but comfortable. Great experiences, no debt.

## Booking Strategy

I book in stages:

1. Flights first. Lock in dates.
2. Accommodation for first 2-3 nights. Not the whole trip.
3. Rest of accommodation I book as I travel.

Gives flexibility. If I love a place, I stay longer. If not, I move on.

Some people book everything in advance. I prefer flexibility.

## Final Thoughts

Travel doesn't have to be expensive. With planning and flexibility, you can see the world on a budget.

The secret: avoid tourist prices. Stay where locals stay. Eat where locals eat. Travel when others don't.

You'll spend less, see more, experience more.

Some of my best memories are from budget trips. Street food in Bangkok. Walking through small towns in Portugal. Chatting with locals at a cafe in Budapest.

Expensive doesn't mean better. Sometimes cheaper is actually better.
