Large language models. GPT, Claude, Gemini. Everyone talks about them.

But what are they actually? Let me explain simply.

## What Is a Large Language Model?

A large language model (LLM) is a program that generates text.

But not just any text. Text that sounds human. Text that makes sense. Text that answers questions, writes essays, codes programs.

**How simple to think about it:**

Imagine reading billions of pages of text. Every book, article, website. You'd notice patterns. How words follow words. How sentences structure. How arguments build.

LLMs do this at massive scale. They've "read" enormous amounts of text. They've learned patterns.

When you ask a question, they generate text following patterns they've learned.

That's it. Pattern matching at massive scale.

## How Do They Work?

**Training:**

LLMs are trained on huge amounts of text. Books, websites, articles, conversations.

During training, the model learns to predict: what word comes next?

"Once upon a ____"

Model predicts "time" is most likely. Or "a" or "the."

This seems simple. But with enough text, the model learns:
- Grammar
- Facts
- Reasoning patterns
- Writing styles
- Conversational flow

**Parameters:**

Models have parameters (think of them as settings). Billions of them.

GPT-4 has estimated 1+ trillion parameters. These parameters encode what the model learned during training.

More parameters = more patterns learned = more capable model.

**Inference:**

When you chat with an LLM, it's doing next-word prediction repeatedly.

You: "What's the capital of France?"

Model generates word by word: "The" "capital" "of" "France" "is" "Paris."

Each word predicted based on all previous words.

## Why Are They Called "Large"?

Two reasons:

**Large data:**

Training data is massive. Trillions of words.

**Large parameters:**

Billions to trillions of parameters.

Earlier language models had millions of parameters. These new ones have billions. Hence "large" language models.

## What Can They Do?

**Answer questions:**

Ask anything. Model generates an answer.

**Write text:**

Essays, emails, articles, stories. Give a topic, get text.

**Code:**

Describe what you want. Model writes code.

**Summarize:**

Give long text. Model summarizes.

**Translate:**

Between languages. Not perfectly, but well.

**Analyze:**

Give data or text. Model analyzes and explains.

**Chat:**

Have conversations. Back and forth dialogue.

## What Can't They Do?

**They don't understand:**

Models generate text based on patterns. They don't truly understand meaning.

Ask about logic puzzles, you'll see. They often fail at reasoning that requires true understanding.

**They hallucinate:**

Models generate plausible-sounding text. Sometimes it's wrong but sounds confident.

Ask for facts, and you might get invented facts. Always verify.

**They have no memory:**

Each conversation starts fresh. Model doesn't remember previous conversations.

(Chat history within a conversation works, but not across sessions.)

**They have knowledge cutoff:**

Training data has a cutoff date. Models don't know recent events unless they have web access.

**They can't learn in real-time:**

If you tell a model something, it doesn't "remember" for next time. It's not learning from your conversation.

## Common Terms Explained

**Token:**

Text is broken into tokens. Roughly, a token is about 4 characters or 0.75 words.

"Hello world" might be 2 tokens.

Models have token limits. GPT-4 has 128K token context window.

**Context window:**

How much text the model can process at once. Larger context = more text processed.

Claude has 200K token context window. That's about 150,000 words.

**Temperature:**

Setting that controls randomness. Low temperature = more predictable. High temperature = more creative/random.

**Fine-tuning:**

Training a model further on specific data. Makes it better at specific tasks.

**Prompt:**

What you give the model. Your question, request, instruction.

**Completion:**

What the model generates. Its response.

European company. Efficient models. Open weights.

## How to Use Them?

**Web interfaces:**

- ChatGPT (OpenAI)
- Claude.ai (Anthropic)
- Gemini (Google)

Just go to the website and chat. No setup needed.

**API:**

For developers, integrate into applications. Pay per use.

**Local:**

Run open-source models on your own hardware. Requires technical knowledge.

## Are They Intelligent?

Debated question.

**Arguments for yes:**

- They reason through problems
- They generate creative outputs
- They pass tests (bar exam, medical boards, etc.)
- They hold conversations

**Arguments for no:**

- They don't understand, just predict
- They make basic reasoning errors
- They can't learn or adapt
- They have no consciousness

My take: They're impressive text generators. Whether that's "intelligence" depends on your definition. But they're useful regardless of the label.

## Will They Replace Jobs?

Some jobs affected more than others:

**High impact:**

- Routine writing
- Basic coding
- Data entry
- Simple research
- Customer support

**Low impact:**

- Physical labor
- Complex human interaction
- Creative direction
- Strategic decisions
- Jobs requiring physical presence

Most likely: jobs change rather than disappear. AI handles routine tasks. Humans handle judgment, creativity, relationships.

Field moves fast. What's true today may change next month.

## Common Misconceptions

**"AI is sentient"**

No evidence of sentience. They generate text. That's it.

**"AI is always right"**

No, they hallucinate. Always verify important information.

**"AI will take over the world"**

Concerns about AI impact are valid. But sci-fi scenarios of AI takeover are speculation.

**"AI is just a chatbot"**

Current interfaces are chat. But models can do more: analyze data, write code, process documents.

**"AI is magic"**

It's math and statistics at scale. Impressive, but not magic.
