Options trading always seemed complex to me. All those terms - calls, puts, strike prices, expiration dates. Intimidating.

But I eventually learned. Here's what I wish someone had told me when starting.

## What Are Options?

Simply put, an option is a contract. You pay a premium for the right to buy or sell stock at a set price by a set date.

Think of it like booking a hotel. You pay a deposit to reserve a room at today's price. Later, you can choose to take the room or not. Either way, you lose the deposit.

Options work similarly. You pay a premium for the right, not obligation, to buy or sell.

## Two Types of Options

**Call Options:**

Call options give you the right to buy.

If you think a stock will go up, you buy a call option. Say Apple is at $150. You buy a call with $160 strike price expiring in a month, paying $5 premium.

If Apple hits $180, your option is worth at least $20 (180 minus 160 strike). You paid $5, made $15 profit. That's 300% return.

If Apple stays below $160, your option expires worthless. You lose your $5 premium.

**Put Options:**

Put options give you the right to sell.

If you think a stock will drop, you buy a put. Same Apple at $150. You buy a $140 strike put, pay $4 premium.

If Apple drops to $120, your put is worth $20 (140 minus 120). You paid $4, made $16 profit. That's 400% return.

If Apple stays above $140, you lose the $4 premium.

## Why Trade Options?

**Leverage:**

Options let you control more shares with less money.

One Apple share at $150 costs $150. One option contract (100 shares) might cost $500 premium. You control $15,000 worth of shares for $500.

If the stock moves your way, percentage returns are huge.

**Risk Defined:**

When buying options, your max loss is the premium paid.

Unlike stocks where you can lose much more than expected, options have a ceiling on losses.

**Flexibility:**

You can profit from stocks going up or down. Stocks only make money when prices rise.

## What I Got Wrong

My first few options trades? All losses.

Mistakes I made:

**Buying Too Close to Expiration:**

My first option expired in 3 days. Stock moved my direction, but not fast enough. Option expired worthless.

Now I buy options with at least 2-4 weeks until expiration. More time for the trade to work.

**Buying Out of the Money:**

I bought cheap options with strike prices far from current price. Felt like a bargain.

Reality: these options usually expire worthless. The probability of stock moving that far is low.

Now I buy in-the-money or at-the-money options. More expensive, but higher probability of profit.

**Not Understanding Implied Volatility:**

Options prices depend on expected future volatility. High IV means expensive options, low IV means cheap.

I bought options when IV was high. Stock moved my direction, but option price barely changed because IV dropped.

Now I check IV before buying. High IV? Options expensive, be careful.

## My Approach Now

After losing money learning, here's my current approach.

**Only Trade What I Understand:**

I only trade options on stocks I follow closely. If I don't know the company well, I don't trade options on it.

**Longer Expiration:**

Minimum 2-4 weeks out, often 1-2 months. Gives time for the thesis to play out.

**Smaller Position Size:**

Options are risky. I limit each trade to 2-5% of my portfolio. Even if I lose, it won't devastate me.

**Have an Exit Plan:**

Before entering, I know my profit target and stop loss.

If option doubles, I often sell half and let the rest ride. If it drops 50%, I exit.

## Paper Trading First

If you're new to options, paper trade first.

Most brokers offer paper trading accounts. Practice with fake money, learn how options work, make mistakes without losing real money.

I paper traded for 3 months before using real money. Saved me from many expensive lessons.

## Greeks You Should Know

Options have "Greeks" - measures of different risks.

**Delta:** How much option price moves when stock moves $1. Delta 0.5 means option moves $0.50 when stock moves $1.

**Theta:** Time decay. Options lose value as expiration approaches. Theta shows how much value lost daily.

**Gamma:** How fast delta changes. Important for understanding how your position's risk changes.

**Vega:** How much option price changes with volatility.

Don't need to be experts, but understanding these helps.

## Common Strategies

I keep it simple. Just buy calls or puts based on directional views.

But there are complex strategies like:

Covered calls - own stock, sell calls against it for income.

Protective puts - own stock, buy puts for downside protection.

Iron condors - profit if stock stays in a range.

These require more experience. I stick with simple directional bets.

## The Truth About Options

Most options traders lose money. Studies show 80-90% of retail options traders are unprofitable.

Why? Too much leverage, not enough understanding, treating options like gambling.

Options can be useful tools. But respect the risks. Start small, learn slowly, don't bet more than you can afford to lose.

I've been trading options for 2 years. Still learning. Still making mistakes sometimes. But overall profitable now.

The key? Patience, education, and risk management. No shortcuts.
