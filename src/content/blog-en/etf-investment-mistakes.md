Honestly, when I started ETF investing, I knew nothing. Just heard "buy ETF steady profit," jumped in.

Result? First year lost 15%.

Looking back now, those mistakes were classic. Today sharing my real experience, hoping to help other beginners.

## Mistake 1: Chasing Hotspots

2021, tech stocks crazy growth. QQQ (Nasdaq 100 ETF) hitting new highs daily. I got excited, bought directly.

Two months later, started dropping. Panicked, sold at 10% loss.

Later learned: QQQ's fastest growth often highest risk moment. Chasing high selling low, beginner's classic mistake.

**What I learned:**
Hotspot doesn't mean good opportunity. Everyone buying, maybe already overpriced.

Now I check valuation. QQQ tech companies, is PE reasonable? Overall market at high point?

Don't chase hotspots. Returns more stable.

## Mistake 2: Not Knowing What I Bought

Initially bought SPY (S&P 500 ETF), only knew "US index."

Later researched, found SPY's biggest holdings: Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Tesla. Tech giants. Tech up, SPY up; tech down, SPY down too.

Thought buying "entire US economy," actually buying tech stocks.

Not SPY bad, but need know what you're buying. If think tech stocks dropping, buy SPY for risk avoidance? Useless, because it IS tech.

**What I learned:**
Every ETF has constituent details. Before buying, check what it holds.

Like avoiding tech risk, check XLE (Energy ETF), XLF (Financial ETF). These sectors totally different from SPY.

## Mistake 3: Thinking Diversification = Safety

Initially bought many ETFs: SPY, QQQ, IWM (small cap), EFA (international). Thought diversified = safe.

Later found: market crashes, all drop together. "Diversification" completely failed during crisis.

Why? Behind companies, many businesses related. Tech drops, big companies drop, small companies drop too. International markets highly correlated with US stocks.

Real diversification needs low correlation assets.

**My adjusted allocation:**
- Stock ETFs: 60%
- Bond ETF (BND): 30%
- Other (Gold ETF like GLD): 10%

Market drops, bonds and gold might rise, hedging some risk.

Ratio varies by person. Key is understanding "diversification" true meaning - not many ETFs, but low correlation assets.

## Mistake 4: Frequent Trading

First year I traded constantly. News says economy bad, sell quickly. Some stock up, buy corresponding ETF.

Year ended, dozens of trades. Besides losing money, paid much fees and taxes.

Biggest frequent trading problem: can't predict. Market rise/fall reasons complex. One news, one data, can't determine direction.

Each trade costs. Guess right earn 5%, costs might eat 1%. Guess wrong lose 5%, plus costs lose 6%. Long-term, frequent traders mostly lose.

**What I learned:**
Buy and hold. Choose good ETFs, hold long-term. Don't buy/sell for short-term swings.

Now annually adjust allocation ratio once or twice. Daily ups/downs ignored, let market run itself.

## Mistake 5: Ignoring Fees

Initially didn't check fees. Bought ETF, later found management fee 1.2%. Yearly, fees eat 1.2% returns.

If returns 8%, fees 1.2%, actual 6.8%. Long-term, this gap huge.

10 years later, 8% compound = 115%, 6.8% compound = only 96%. 19% difference, maybe thousands of dollars.

**What I learned:**
Check expense ratio before buying ETF.

- SPY: 0.09%
- VOO: 0.03% (Same S&P 500, lower fee)
- QQQ: 0.20%

Same index, choose lower fee. Long-term, saved money significant.

## How I Buy ETFs Now?

After mistakes, my strategy quite simple:

**Regular investing VOO**

Monthly fixed date buy VOO (Vanguard S&P 500 ETF). Regardless market ups/downs, buy as planned.

Average cost method benefit: won't buy at highest point. Long-term, costs average out.

**Bond allocation**

Use BND (Total Bond ETF) for risk hedging. Market crashes, bonds might rise, buffer impact.

**Small sector ETFs**

Occasionally buy sector ETFs like XLV (Healthcare), XLE (Energy). But under 10% ratio, not heavy sector bets.

**No complex strategies**

Leveraged ETFs, inverse ETFs, I completely avoid. Seems amplifying returns, actually huge risk. Normal investors playing this, likely lose money.

## Final Thoughts on Mindset

Investing hardest isn't technique, it's mindset.

Market drops 20%, can you resist selling? Everyone panic selling, can you calmly hold?

First year I couldn't. Dropped, panicked, sold. Sold then regretted, regretted then chased back. Back and forth, lost big.

Now mindset stable. 30% drop I sleep fine, knowing long-term market recovers. As long as broad index (like S&P 500), not some junk stock, time solves it.

This mindset not from reading articles. Experienced several drops personally.

So my suggestion: start investing early, experience volatility early. Amount can be small, but must feel personally. Theory never teaches.
