Making 5000 a month in a city where rent alone is 2000. Seems impossible to save, right?

Three years ago, I thought so too. Now I have 50,000 saved.

Here's how I did it.

## The Numbers First

My situation: 5000 monthly salary, expensive city.

**Monthly Expenses:**
- Rent: 2000 (shared apartment)
- Food: 600
- Transportation: 150
- Utilities and phone: 150
- Miscellaneous: 200
- Total: 3100

**Monthly Saving:** 1900

Three years, 1900 x 36 = 68,400.

But raises and bonuses added more. Final total: about 50,000 after some expenses I didn't plan for.

That's the simple version. Reality was messier.

## How I Kept Rent Low

Rent was my biggest expense. 2000 is actually cheap for my city. Here's how.

**Shared Housing:**

I live with roommates. Three people in a three-bedroom apartment.

Living alone would cost 3000+. With roommates, 2000.

Roommates have downsides. Less privacy, shared spaces. But saving 1000+ monthly is worth it.

**Location:**

I don't live in city center. 30-minute commute, but rent is lower.

Living centrally would add 500-1000 to rent. I chose commute time over location.

**Modest Apartment:**

Not fancy. Older building. Small room. But clean and safe.

I looked at many apartments. Chose function over style.

## Food on a Budget

Food is my second biggest expense. I keep it to 600 monthly.

**Cook at Home:**

Eating out is expensive. One restaurant meal costs as much as home-cooked meals for two days.

I cook 90% of my meals. Breakfast at home. Lunch prepared the night before. Dinner at home.

**Meal Prep:**

Sundays, I prepare lunches for the week. Rice, protein, vegetables. Simple meals.

No daily decision about lunch. Just grab and go.

**Limit Eating Out:**

I eat out once per week as a treat. Not more.

If friends want to meet for dinner, I suggest alternatives. Parks, home cooking, cheaper spots.

**Buy Smart:**

I shop at discount stores. Buy in bulk. Choose seasonal produce.

Brand names don't matter much for basics. Rice, pasta, vegetables - store brands are fine.

## Transportation Savings

**Public Transit:**

I don't own a car. Monthly transit pass costs 150.

Car would cost: payment, insurance, gas, parking, maintenance. Easily 500+ monthly.

Public transit is slower but much cheaper.

**Walk When Possible:**

I walk anywhere within 30 minutes. Saves money, good exercise.

## What I Don't Spend On

This is where real savings happen. I don't spend on things many people consider essential.

**No Car:**

Already mentioned. But worth emphasizing. Car is a massive expense.

I calculated: car would cost me 500-700 monthly. That's 6000-8400 yearly.

I use ride-sharing when necessary. Maybe 50 monthly. Far cheaper than owning.

**No Subscriptions:**

No streaming services. No gym membership I don't use. No monthly boxes.

If I want to watch something, there are free options. Exercise: running and home workouts.

Monthly subscriptions add up. 50 here, 10 there - easily 100+ monthly.

**Minimal Shopping:**

I buy clothes when needed, not wanted. Last year I bought: two work shirts, one pair of shoes. That's it.

I don't follow fashion. My clothes are basic, functional, last years.

**Limited Entertainment:**

Movies, concerts, events - I do these occasionally but not often.

Free entertainment: parks, hiking, reading, online content. Plenty to do without spending.

**Old Phone:**

I use a 3-year-old phone. Works fine. I don't need the newest model.

Keeping a phone 3+ years instead of upgrading annually saves thousands over time.

## The Saving System

Saving isn't about willpower. It's about systems.

**Pay Yourself First:**

On payday, savings leaves my account immediately. Automatic transfer to savings account.

I never see this money. Can't spend what's not there.

**Separate Accounts:**

Checking account for spending. Savings account for savings. Investment account for investing.

Money in checking gets spent. Money in savings stays.

**Emergency Fund First:**

First 6 months of saving went to emergency fund. 10,000.

This fund prevents debt when unexpected expenses hit. Car repair, medical, job loss.

**Then Invest:**

After emergency fund, I started investing. Monthly amount to index funds.

Investing forces me to not touch the money. Investments are for long-term.

## Mindset Shifts

Saving isn't just tactics. It's mindset.

**Needs vs Wants:**

Before any purchase, I ask: need or want?

Needs: rent, food, transportation. Wants: most everything else.

I still spend on wants occasionally. But consciously, not automatically.

**Lifestyle Inflation:**

When I got raises, I didn't increase spending.

Raise means more saving, not more spending.

First year salary: 4500. Third year: 5500. Savings rate increased.

**Friends and Spending:**

Some friends spend freely. Dinner at expensive restaurants. Weekend trips. Shopping.

I can't keep up. I don't try.

Real friends accept your financial situation. I'm honest: "I'm on a budget."

We find cheaper ways to spend time together.

**Comparison is the Thief of Joy:**

Social media shows everyone's highlight reels. Expensive vacations. New cars. Nice apartments.

Comparison makes me feel poor and unsatisfied.

I stopped comparing. My journey is my journey. Saving is my goal.

## What I Splurge On

I'm not a miser. I spend on things that matter to me.

**Travel:**

Once per year, I take a trip. Budget travel, but I go.

I save for this. It's planned. Not impulse.

**Health:**

I spend on quality food. Don't skimp on nutrition.

Health is an investment, not an expense.

**Experiences with Friends:**

I'll spend for meaningful time with friends. Not expensive restaurants, but occasional activities.

Relationships matter more than saving every dollar.

## Mistakes I Made

Not everything went smoothly.

**Unrealistic Budgets:**

Early budgets were too strict. No room for anything fun.

Result: I'd break the budget, feel guilty, give up.

Now: I budget for fun. Small amount, but intentional.

**Not Tracking:**

I thought I knew where money went. I didn't.

Tracking revealed I was spending 400 on food, not 300 as I estimated.

Now I track everything. App makes it easy.

**Trying to Keep Up:**

When friends bought new phones, I wanted one too.

Spent money I shouldn't have. Regretted it.

Learned: my financial goals are mine. Others' choices aren't my concern.

**Too Aggressive Investing:**

Tried some risky investments. Lost money.

Now I stick with boring index funds. Slow and steady.

## Three Years Later

Started with nothing. Now:

- Emergency fund: 10,000
- Investments: 40,000
- Total: 50,000

Not wealthy. But secure.

Before, one unexpected expense meant debt. Now, I can handle emergencies.

Before, I had no financial future. Now, compound interest is working for me.

Before, money was stress. Now, money is managed.

## Is This Sustainable?

Some say this lifestyle is deprivation. It's not sustainable long-term.

Actually, I've found it sustainable. Because it's not deprivation.

I cook good food. I have a comfortable home. I see friends. I travel once yearly.

What I don't have is: new cars, expensive clothes, daily takeout, constant entertainment.

That's not deprivation. That's choices.

The key is: I'm saving for goals. Not just hoarding money.

Goals: financial independence, home ownership, retirement.

These goals make saving feel purposeful, not restrictive.

## If You're Starting

If you're making 5000 or similar, here's where to start.

**Track Your Spending:**

One month, track every dollar. See where money actually goes.

Most people are surprised. I was.

**Cut the Biggest Expenses:**

Focus on housing, transportation, food. These are the big numbers.

Small cuts (canceling Netflix) matter less than big cuts (cheaper apartment).

**Build an Emergency Fund:**

Before anything else, save 3-6 months of expenses.

This fund is freedom. It means one problem doesn't create debt.

**Automate Savings:**

Set up automatic transfer on payday. Don't rely on willpower.

**Increase Income:**

This article focuses on saving. But increasing income matters too.

Side jobs, raises, new skills. More income = more potential savings.

The key: spend intentionally, save automatically, think long-term.

Start where you are. Use what you have. Do what you can.

Every dollar saved is a dollar working for your future.
